Ownership & Wear Patterns
How participants relate to each of the 13 garment types, from actively worn to discarded or never owned.
Overall Ownership Distribution
Key Insights
38% of all garment interactions are never-owned, the largest single category. This is a useful baseline showing which items simply aren't part of participants' wardrobes.
32% of items are actively worn (wear-often), while 22% are kept but rarely worn.
Only 8% of items have been discarded, but this likely underrepresents real discard behaviour, discarded items are harder for participants to recall and log.

Key Insights
63% of owned garments were bought new, with secondhand purchase at just 13%. Gifts account for 15%, suggesting social gifting is a significant inflow channel.
Price distribution is relatively even across brackets (€1–20 through €100+), suggesting participants represent a range of spending behaviours rather than a single profile.
The majority of owned items are 1–4 years old (69%). Items older than 5 years are rare, either participants have newer wardrobes, or older items have already been discarded.

Section 3 · Garment Lifecycle
Why Items Go Unworn
Reasons given by participants for items in the rarely-wear category (50 items, 35% answer rate).
Reasons for Rarely Wearing
Interpretation
Seasonality is the dominant barrier: 58% of unworn items are simply out-of-season, not permanently abandoned. These items may have high reactivation potential.
"Forgot" is the second reason (18%), tied with out-of-style. Forgotten items represent a real design opportunity, a reminder or rediscovery (reflection) could reactivate them.
Fit issues account for 6%: a smaller but notable category where the item is genuinely no longer usable for its owner. These are strong discard candidates.
Section 4 · Prototype Performance
Survey Completion & Data Quality (User Insights)
How well the Wardrobe Mirror prototype performed as a data collection tool. 39 participants · 9 garment types per full session.
Completion Distribution
Key Observations
21 of 39 participants (54%) completed all 9 garment cards. This is a reasonable rate for a first prototype run, but indicates drop-off is significant.
10 participants completed only 1 item, a hard drop-off that suggests the entry experience may cause early abandonment before the participant is engaged.
A notable bimodal distribution: either participants complete everything or they drop off very early. There is almost no "middle engagement", suggesting the tool either hooks people or loses them immediately. "Note for Us: Do we need to Fix?"
Question Answer Rates: Owned Items Only (142 records)
Critical data gaps: Future Plan (2%), Why Discard (2%), Care Instructions (9%), and Repair History (8%) are almost entirely unanswered. These were either not surfaced clearly in the interface or participants skipped them consistently.
Summary
What This Data Tells Us
High-level takeaways for the Wardrobe Mirror research & design team.
Garment Lifecycle Findings
Seasonal wardrobing is real. Most rarely-worn items are set aside seasonally, not permanently discarded. Design interventions around seasonal transitions could be high-impact.
Graduation Robe is the clearest lifecycle case. High discard rate, short active use, often received as a gift, a textbook single-use garment with low reactivation probability. "Note: Life Changing Values :) LOL"
Denim Jacket shows wardrobe inertia. Owned by nearly everyone, but the most rarely-worn item (10 cases). Participants keep it but don't wear it, a classic "sentimental keep" pattern.
Black Jeans are the most actively worn. 17 wear-often vs. 0 rarely-wear, the highest utility/retention ratio of any item. A useful benchmark for high-engagement garments.
Prototype Recommendations
Re-examine the entry flow. 10/39 participants dropped off after one garment card, this pattern needs to be understood before scaling data collection.
Future Plan, Care, and Repair questions are broken. <10% answer rates suggest these are either hidden, confusing, or feel too effortful within the game flow. Redesign or reposition these questions.
Frequency data is useful but partial. 51% response rate for wear frequency, better than most other fields, but still suggests participants skip it when the item is rarely worn (no frequency to report).
The core data (price, age, inflow) is solid. 100% completion on these three fields confirms the main garment card mechanics work well. Build on this foundation.
